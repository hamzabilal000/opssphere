Multi-Organization Invitations

Since Day 5, inviting someone whose email already had an OpsSphere account into a second organization failed with a flat 409 - a real, disclosed limitation, not a bug. Today closes it, without ever touching what an invitation IS - only what accepting one can now mean.

The one-paragraph summary

Day 5's createOrgInvitation rejected outright the moment it saw an existing account for the invited email - "inviting existing users into a second organization isn't supported yet," its own comment admitted. The reason was really in acceptInvitation: it always creates a brand-new User document, with no path for "this person already has a password, just add them to this org." Today adds that second path as its own endpoint, acceptInvitationAsExistingUser - no new password, no new account, just one more Membership row - and teaches the invitation-creation step to stop blocking existing users in the first place.

Same rule as always Every changed file has comments explaining what's new - especially auth.service.ts's new acceptInvitationAsExistingUser and AcceptInvitationPage.tsx's branching logic, which is where almost all of today's actual thinking shows up.

Three new concepts, explained simply

1. Two different questions were tangled into one 409

Day 5's single check actually covered two SEPARATE questions at once: "does this email already have an account?" and "would this invitation actually be useful?" Today untangles them. createOrgInvitation now only cares about the second one - is this email already a member of THIS specific organization? (still a 409 if so). Whether the email has an account AT ALL is no longer creation's problem; it becomes ACCEPTANCE's problem instead, decided the moment someone actually opens the invitation link.

2. One boolean tells the frontend which of two entirely different flows to show

InvitationPreview gained one new field: accountExists. A brand-new email still gets the original Day 3/5 experience - type a password, get an account, get logged in automatically. An email that already has an account gets something with a completely different shape: no password field at all, just a check of "are you ALREADY logged in as that account right now?" (via the same useMeQuery hook every other page uses) and, if so, one button that just adds the missing Membership.

3. Being logged in already IS the proof of identity - nothing invitation-specific needed

The original /accept route proves identity through the invitation TOKEN itself (only the real recipient could have clicked the emailed link). The new /accept-existing route proves identity a completely different way: it's gated by the exact same requireAuth middleware every other protected route in the app uses, and then does ONE extra check - does the CURRENTLY logged-in account's email match the invitation's email, exactly? If someone is logged in as the wrong account, they're told plainly to log out and back in as the right one; the token itself isn't consumed by that failed attempt, so trying again with the correct account works.

What actually happens, step by step

1
An org admin invites bob@example.com - who already has an OpsSphere account from a DIFFERENT organization - into theirs. createOrgInvitation no longer blocks this; it only checks Bob isn't already a member of THIS org.
2
Bob gets the same invitation email as always and opens the link. getInvitationPreview now also checks whether an account exists for that email and includes accountExists: true in its response.
3
AcceptInvitationPage sees accountExists and renders the SECOND flow - no password field, just a message and (if Bob's already logged in as himself) a "Join" button.
4
If Bob isn't currently logged in (or is logged into some OTHER account), the page tells him to log in as bob@example.com and come back to this same link - the invitation token isn't consumed by any of this, so revisiting it is always safe.
5
Once logged in as himself, clicking "Join" calls the new POST .../accept-existing route - it re-verifies his session's email matches the invitation, creates the Membership, marks the invitation accepted, and sends him to the dashboard. No new account, no new password, ever.

A design detail worth understanding: why acceptance needed a whole SEPARATE route

Folding this into the existing /accept route would have made it do two jobs It would have been possible to keep one endpoint and branch INSIDE it on whether a password was provided. That was deliberately rejected: the original /accept is public (no login required - the invitee doesn't have an account yet, that's the whole point) and its job is CREATING an account. The new route is the opposite in every dimension - it REQUIRES being logged in already, and its job is exclusively adding a Membership, never touching the User collection at all. Two routes with two different middleware chains and two single-purpose service functions is more code, but each one stays honestly simple - matching this project's running preference for a few small, obvious functions over one function that branches its way through multiple unrelated responsibilities.

What was deliberately left simple for today

  • No "redirect back here after login" wiring on LoginPage.tsx - someone who isn't logged in is told to log in and then reopen the SAME invitation link by hand. A `?next=` redirect param would be a reasonable small polish upgrade, not required to prove the underlying feature.
  • Dropping an invitation onto an account logged in as the WRONG email shows a plain instruction to log out and back in - no automatic account-switcher UI. OpsSphere doesn't have a "switch account without a full logout" feature yet at all, so this matches how account-switching works everywhere else in the app today.
  • The invitation EMAIL itself still reads the same regardless of which flow the recipient will end up seeing - it doesn't say "you already have an account" up front. The page figures that out once they click through, which is simpler than maintaining two email templates for one event.

How to try it yourself

  1. Run pnpm install - no new packages today.
  2. Create two organizations, both owned by the same admin account for simplicity.
  3. From Organization A, invite an email that's already a MEMBER of Organization B.
  4. Open the invitation link while logged OUT - confirm it tells you to log in as that email first, with no password field shown at all.
  5. Log in as that member, then reopen the exact same invitation link - confirm a "Join Organization A" button appears, and clicking it adds you without ever touching your password.
  6. Confirm the org switcher (Day 6's Sidebar) now shows both organizations for that account.
